The Problem

⚠️
BSMILE Flyover Plan — SV Road to Silk Board

Bengaluru Smart Infrastructure Ltd (BSMILE) has floated tenders for an 11.6-km elevated corridor connecting SV Road Metro to Silk Board via Koramangala and Indiranagar at a cost of ₹1,300 crore. DPR is underway as part of a broader plan for 13 elevated corridors totalling 126 km city-wide.

The Inner Ring Road corridor through Indiranagar, Domlur, and Koramangala is one of the most congested stretches in Bengaluru. It connects major employment hubs, residential areas, and transit nodes. A flyover here might offer short-term relief — but it comes at a permanent and irreversible cost.

Why a Flyover Blocks Metro Forever

A metro station requires substantial column foundations — both for elevated structures and for underground cut-and-cover sections. If flyover pillars are sunk along the Inner Ring Road alignment, there is no space remaining for metro columns or structural excavation. The corridor would be physically locked out of any future metro.

Underground metro through this zone isn't viable either once flyover pillars are in — the weight loading of the flyover structure above compromises underground construction feasibility and soil bearing capacity. This is a one-way door.

📌
Current Status: Shelved

Despite IISc's recommendation and inclusion in Bengaluru's 2020 Comprehensive Mobility Plan, the Inner Ring Metro has been indefinitely shelved in Phase 3 planning, with focus shifted to peripheral corridors. The BSMILE flyover plan would make revival impossible.

The Phase 3A Sarjapur–Hebbal (Red Line) already goes underground through Koramangala — proving that underground construction in this exact zone is accepted, feasible, and already planned. The IRM's underground alignment through the same area was therefore not structurally impossible — it was a policy decision to shelve it.

The Proposal

Building an entire new metro line has enormous challenges — land acquisition, new depots, new rolling stock, budget, years of planning. But extending an existing line is far more tractable. Here's a two-step proposal that uses existing infrastructure creatively.

Step 1 — Extend Blue Line onto Yellow Line Infrastructure

The Blue Line runs ~58 km from Kempegowda International Airport to Silk Board. The Yellow Line runs 19.15 km from RV Road to Bommasandra, with a depot at Hebbagodi.

The Blue and Yellow lines share a possible interchange alignment at Silk Board. The Blue Line can be extended onto Yellow Line infrastructure from Silk Board → RV Road, effectively using Yellow's existing tracks. This extension connects the airport end-to-end through the Electronic City IT corridor — a highly logical and demand-heavy route.

✅
Why this works

The Yellow Line's western terminus at RV Road has no forward scope — the Green Line already serves that region. Blue Line using Yellow's Silk Board→RV Road segment gives the Blue Line a complete south-end terminus and makes Yellow Line's western leg redundant, freeing it to go elsewhere.

Step 2 — Reroute Yellow Line Northward via Inner Ring Road

  1. 1
    Silk Board (Interchange with Blue + Red) Yellow Line now starts here, freed from going west to RV Road. Shares platforms with Blue Line and connects to Phase 3A Red Line.
  2. 2
    Koramangala Sweeps northeast into Koramangala — a high-density commercial and residential zone with zero current metro access. Meets the proposed Red Line (Phase 3A Sarjapur–Hebbal).
  3. 3
    Inner Ring Road → Domlur Follows the Inner Ring Road corridor through Domlur — a major employment hub between Koramangala and Indiranagar that is currently completely unserved by metro.
  4. 4
    Indiranagar 100 Feet Road Passes through Indiranagar's commercial spine — one of Bengaluru's most congested and economically active corridors. Connects to 80ft Road and CMH Road catchment.
  5. 5
    SV Road Metro + SVMT Connects to the existing SV Road Metro station and Swami Vivekananda Metro Terminal — a critical north-south interchange node.
  6. 6
    Kalyan Nagar / Hennur (Terminus) Line terminates at Kalyan Nagar or Hennur — expanding metro coverage into the growing northeast residential belt, providing a depot site and turnaround facility.

Anticipating Counter-Arguments

"Blue Line can't run on Yellow Line tracks"

Response: Namma Metro Phase 2 uses standard gauge (1435mm) across all lines. Track gauge is compatible. The more substantive question is signalling integration — BMRCL would need to confirm whether CBTC systems across both lines can share the Silk Board→RV Road segment. This is an operational engineering question, not a fundamental impossibility. Codesharing or joint operation exists in many metro systems globally.

"Where does the Yellow Line depot go?"

Response: The current Hebbagodi depot sits at the southern end of the Yellow Line near Bommasandra. Under this proposal, Blue Line would inherit Yellow's southern infra and could potentially use Hebbagodi for stabling. The rerouted Yellow Line would need a new depot — Kalyan Nagar or Hennur, where land is relatively more available, is the natural candidate. This is the main open question in the proposal.

"Underground construction is too expensive here"

Response: Phase 3A (Red Line: Sarjapur–Hebbal) is already planned underground through Koramangala — the same zone. If underground construction were cost-prohibitive here, BMRCL would not have approved Phase 3A. The IRM study estimated ₹10,000 crore for 34 km — comparable per-km cost to other underground metro projects in India.

"Flyovers actually reduce congestion"

Response: Induced demand is well-documented in urban transport literature. Elevated corridors increase road capacity temporarily, attracting more vehicles, restoring (and often worsening) congestion within years. The IISc study showed the Inner Ring Metro would reduce road traffic by 22% — a flyover in the same corridor has no equivalent long-term evidence base.

🌳
Tree cover — an additional angle

Indiranagar is a tree-lined, heritage neighbourhood. The BSMILE flyover DPR already faces public pushback over tree cover loss. The metro proposal — if underground — preserves every tree. This is a powerful public sentiment argument for the Twitter thread.
